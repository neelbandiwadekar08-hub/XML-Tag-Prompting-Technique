The Problem

One paragraph means Claude is guessing

Every unstructured prompt forces Claude to silently answer three questions before it can even start. Get any of them wrong and you get a generic response.

Without XML tags
I work in a factory and we keep having quality issues on the line, three machines failed twice this week and the supervisor doesn't know why. Can you help figure out what's wrong? Claude asks itself: → Who should I be? → What exactly is the task? → What should the answer look like?
With XML tags
<role>Manufacturing quality engineer</role> <context> 3 machines failed twice this week. No confirmed root cause yet. </context> <instructions> 1. Diagnose root causes. 2. Recommend fixes. 3. Suggest prevention. </instructions> <output_format> Root Causes · Fixes · Prevention </output_format>
The Method

Five tags. Each one has a single job.

The tag names are not fixed. Use whatever fits your domain and stay consistent across prompts.

<role>
Who Claude Is
Sets the expertise and perspective for every answer that follows.
Without it: generic, surface-level responses.
<context>
The Situation
Gives Claude the background it needs to understand your specific problem.
Without it: Claude fills gaps with assumptions.
<instructions>
What To Do
Numbered steps telling Claude exactly what to do and in what order.
Without it: Claude decides the scope, not you.
<constraints>
The Rules
Sets the limits so Claude stays practical and on-scope.
Without it: answers that look right but are not usable.
<output_format>
What It Looks Like
Describes the exact shape and structure of the response you want.
Without it: different structure every single time.
Real-World Examples

Three domains. Same five-tag structure.

Click a tab to switch examples. Copy any prompt straight into Claude and replace the context with your own situation.

Situation: Three machines on the assembly line keep failing with no confirmed root cause.

Production Example
<role> You are a manufacturing quality engineer with 10 years of production floor experience. </role> <context> Three machines on our assembly line have each failed twice this week. We suspect operator error or equipment wear but have no confirmed root cause. </context> <instructions> 1. List the three most likely root causes of repeated machine failure. 2. Recommend a short-term workaround for each. 3. Suggest one long-term prevention measure. </instructions> <constraints> - Solutions must be actionable by a team of five operators. - Avoid recommendations that require outside contractors or capital expenditure. </constraints> <output_format> Root Causes (3 bullets) Short-Term Fixes (one per cause, numbered) Long-Term Prevention (1 paragraph) </output_format>
Why this works

The <role> anchors every answer in production-floor thinking, not generic advice. The <constraints> prevent Claude from recommending something impractical like hiring a specialist. The <output_format> gives you the same structure every time you run it.

Situation: A key client's usage has dropped 40% and their contract renews in two weeks.

Sales Example
<role> You are a senior B2B sales coach with experience in SaaS account management. </role> <context> A key client's annual contract renews in two weeks. Their product usage has dropped 40% this quarter and they have not responded to two check-in emails. </context> <instructions> 1. Identify the most likely reasons for reduced engagement. 2. Suggest three talking points to open the renewal conversation. 3. Recommend how to handle a price objection if it comes up. </instructions> <constraints> - Tone must be consultative, not pushy. - Do not suggest discounting before understanding the client's actual concern. </constraints> <output_format> Risk Assessment (2 sentences) Opening Talking Points (3 bullets) Objection Handling Script (short paragraph) </output_format>
Why this works

The <constraints> tag protects the sales strategy. Without it, Claude might jump straight to offering a discount before understanding the real problem. The <output_format> produces something the rep can print and bring to the call.

Situation: A product launch is three weeks behind and leadership has not been updated yet.

Project Management Example
<role> You are a senior project manager communicating with executive stakeholders. </role> <context> A product launch project is three weeks behind schedule due to a delayed vendor integration. The core team is aware but leadership has not been formally updated. </context> <instructions> 1. Draft a concise status update email for senior leadership. 2. Include the reason for the delay without assigning blame. 3. Propose a revised timeline and the key decision needed from leadership. </instructions> <constraints> - Email must be under 150 words. - Tone should be confident and solution-focused, not defensive. </constraints> <output_format> Subject Line Email Body (under 150 words) Decision Required (1 sentence, clearly flagged) </output_format>
Why this works

The <output_format> tag is unusually precise here — under 150 words with a flagged decision line. That level of specificity is exactly what makes the response immediately usable rather than something you have to rewrite before sending.

Save Time

Stop retyping your tag structure every conversation

Every word you type uses part of Claude's context window. Retyping the same five tags in every new chat wastes tokens on setup instead of the actual task.

Use Claude Projects instead

Save your tag structure once under Project Instructions and every chat in that project inherits it automatically.

  • 1Go to claude.ai and click Projects in the left sidebar
  • 2Create a new project and give it a name
  • 3Paste your tag structure into Project Instructions
  • 4Every chat inside that project uses it automatically
